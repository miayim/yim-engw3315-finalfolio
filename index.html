01 intro

What does it mean to be on the threshold?

A short note on why this journal exists and what "liminal" means when applied to a discipline that often resists softness. This is the space between the technical and the lived.

read entry ↓

In a discussion post, my professor Ellen used the word liminal to describe the way my writing occupies the space of thresholds, of being between one thing and another. In engaing in the Preflection for this folio I found that more of my writing this semester orbits around that idea.

CS is often framed as a discipline of precision: correct answers, working code, provable theorems. But so much of what makes it interesting to me lives in the in-between: between a system and the people it shapes. My goal is to explore what it means to be on the threshold between computer science and the human experience.

This journal is my attempt to reflect on my work from the last few months from that threshold. The entries here are pieces I feel fit best in this space. They are technical and personal, analytical and imperfect.

02 piece Mar 11

0, 1, and everything else

On how small design decisions, a form field, a forced binary, can encode assumptions about identity and scale into environments that shape real lives.

read entry ↓

This is the piece that sparked this journal. The threshold here is literal: the gap between a design decision made without consideration and the life of a person (me) that decision touches. I wrote this as a letter to computer scientists, but I think it has value for anyone who participates in systems, whether they build them or not. Because we make decisions that shape the world around us.

To computer scientists:

In computer science, binary is the default.

0s and 1s are the very foundation of every function.

By nature, we are taught to think within rigid boxes with specific notions of truth.

People are made up of many truths.

The body is one kind of truth.

Identity is another.

Most systems are designed under the assumption that those two truths are the same.
But what about when they are not?

The truth of a body I did not choose is kept in databases I have never seen and in code written by people who never considered me. So many of the systems that shape our reality quietly enforce a truth. Right now, many of those systems are built in a way that makes people like me harder to recognize and easier to challenge.

A dropdown menu with two options.

A required field that won't let you proceed.

A scan at the airport going through TSA.

A bathroom.

These are small things. At least they feel small when you build them. Edge cases saved for later.

But systems scale.

And when they do, those small decisions create environments.

In Kansas, a law says that identity must return to the sex assigned at birth. The bathrooms must align with that truth. That someone can take legal action if they feel harmed by your presence alone.

A law like that does not exist in isolation. It relies on systems to enforce it.

In the same way rivers are redirected, contained, and buried beneath cities so that streets can exist where water once flowed freely, identity is redirected, too. Forced into channels that make the system easier to maintain.

But water does not stop existing because you force it down a pipe.

People do not stop existing because a system fails to account for them.

It is easy to think of these problems as strictly social or political issues.
But they are also design problems, which means they have solutions.

That solution, summarized by a single word: consideration.

Design systems with the people who use them in mind.

Design for complexity instead of convenience.

Design for reality instead of assumptions.

Because every dropdown, every policy is making a claim about who is allowed to exist comfortably in the world.

Despite all of this, trans people are still here.

Just as we always have been.

Just as we always will be.

There is something quietly beautiful about that.
To exist without permission and without apology.
To find joy in a world that was not built for you.

It is easy to focus on the things that are hard, defining trans existence with struggle. But what has defined my experiences as a trans person so much more is joy.

It is laughter in spaces that expect silence.

It is a community built where systems have left gaps.

It is choosing yourself, again and again, even when systems refuse to.

Not everyone writes code or passes laws.
But everyone participates in systems, whether they realize it or not.

Anyone can question the defaults.

You can notice who is left out.

And maybe most importantly, you can uplift the people around you.
Make space for everyone to exist loudly.

Because the world changes the same way systems are built, one decision at a time.

03 piece Jan 13

Design choices all the way down

What happens when you approach a text the way you'd approach a proof or a program? On structure, audience, and the surprising overlap between writing and systems thinking.

read entry ↓

This piece sits at the heart of what this journal is trying to do. Writing about reading and doing it through the lens of CS and math helped me see that the two disciplines aren't as separate as they're usually treated. Both are about noticing design, asking why, and learning from what came before you.

Part one

What I liked most about Mike Bunn's How to Read Like a Writer is that it gives a name to something I already do instinctively: considering the choices behind a piece of writing, not just the story or argument itself. Bunn describes reading like a writer as paying attention to how the text was constructed so that you learn how to "build" one for yourself, and that framing really resonated with me. It treats writing less like magic and more like engineering. Something built intentionally, piece by piece, by someone who made a series of decisions.

I feel like this mindset connects strongly to how I already think in math and computer science. In those fields, you're always asking why something was designed a certain way: Why this algorithm? Why this structure? Why this proof strategy? To me, reading like a writer feels similar. It's about noticing the design decisions and thinking through their consequences.

I’ve definitely read like a writer before, both consciously and unconsciously. I feel like it’s something I have always thought about when reading, but it became a bigger habit after I took AP English in high school. In class, we spent time analyzing reading as writers, especially in our poetry and personal essays unit. My teacher taught us to approach readings with themes in mind. I remember reading Invisible Man, focusing on the theme of music. I found that I got much more out of the book while keeping in mind why and how Ralph Ellison was using themes as tools for his writing. We also very intently read like writers when reading personal essays in order to find choices we liked and apply those techniques to our college essays. As a result, I often read with the author's intentions in mind and pay attention to how they use different images, patterns, or moments to build the larger meaning or plot. Sometimes this means noticing repeated symbols, sometimes it’s the pacing of scenes, and sometimes it’s the way a sentence is shaped.

One idea that I have always struggled with when it comes to RLW is getting caught up in what the author did “on purpose” and what might have been accidental. It can be easy to use that as a metric and think that the analysis is only important for choices made intentionally. But Bunn helped me realize that this doesn’t really matter. If something means something to a reader who is reading like a writer, that reader can still learn from it and apply it to their own writing. Even if the author didn’t consciously plan it, the effect is still real and still useful as a model.

Finally, a sentence I liked:

"When you read like a writer, you are trying to figure out how the text you are reading was constructed so that you learn how to 'build' one for yourself."

To me, this frames reading as part of a long chain of progress: learning from people who came before you, studying their methods, and then adapting or improving on them. It’s a very engineering and math-like way to think about writing. You don’t start from nothing; you study existing solutions and learn how they work.

Part Two: How to Read Like a Computer Science and Math Major

Reading like a computer science and math major means reading with an eye for structure, design choices, and purpose.

First, always look for the design choices. Ask: Why did the author structure this explanation this way? Why introduce this concept before that one? Why use this example instead of another? It's like making sure you define a function before calling it. Order matters! In CS and math, every proof, algorithm, or system is the result of tradeoffs. Writing is no different.

Second, remember that a lot of technical writing is written for other people, but not always written as if it were. When reading something technical like a textbook, paper, or documentation, read like someone who might someday have to explain this to someone else. Ask: Where does this get confusing? What assumptions does the author make about the reader? What would I change if I had to teach this?

Third, practice zooming in and zooming out. In computer science and math (and in my opinion, problem solving in general), it is important to remember the bigger picture to make sure you are still moving towards the right end goal:

Zoom in: Look closely at a definition, a paragraph, a line of code, or a single step in a proof. Why is it written this way? Zoom out: Look at the whole structure. How does this section fit into the chapter? How does this idea support the overall goal?

Fourth, think about context. Who is this written for? What does the author assume you already know? What problem is this trying to solve?

Finally, step away and come back. Finally, step away and come back. In math and CS, stepping away from a problem often makes the structure suddenly clearer. The same is true for reading: when you come back, you can often see patterns, gaps, and design decisions you missed the first time.

04 piece Jan 27

Is computer science really about computers?

A look at what CS borrows from other fields and what those fields reveal about CS. Includes a conversation about AI, authorship, and whether a machine can intend something.

read entry ↓

One of the biggest things I've learned over the course of my studies is that computer science is most interesting at its edges where it intersects with other disciplines and has to bend. This piece while about interdisciplinarity, also explores the idea that the problems worth solving are almost always too big for one field.

Part one

After reading sections 1 and 2 of A Time for Interdisciplinarity, I find myself having to agree with the notion that interdisciplinarity and purposefully teaching it in universities are critical. As Tromp puts it: “interdisciplinary collaboration is a prerequisite for science to contribute to major problems – wicked problems – and thus generate impact of knowledge.” I think that all of us here have an advantage in understanding the value of interdisciplinary education, given that we all study at a university that facilitates such learning through combined majors across colleges.

Intentionally leaning in an interdisciplinary way teaches us to speak each other’s academic languages. Perhaps that feels both obvious and hard. Obvious because collaboration always requires communication, but hard because every discipline has its own assumptions, vocabulary, and standards of proof. As a result, learning from different disciplines shows us how to become learners and synthesizers, not just parrots of theorems and definitions we have memorized. We learn how and why different disciplines have different methods rather than getting stuck in one way of seeing the world.

This leads me into another part of the reading I found surprising and thought-provoking. That is the discrepancy between all of the different forms of applying multiple disciplines. I didn’t realize there was such a spectrum: multidisciplinarity, interdisciplinarity, transdisciplinarity, cross-disciplinarity, even postdisciplinarity. I especially liked the idea that interdisciplinarity exists on a continuum, from simple communication between fields all the way to full integration of methods, concepts, and ways of thinking.

In some ways, determining the differences felt trivial, but I think it's actually important to understand them and distinguish between them because it allows us to be more intentional with how we engage in different disciplines and the way we communicate when doing so.

A final quote from the reading:

Interdisciplinarity isn't just a preference — it's something we need when the problem itself is too big for one field.

Part two

My combined major of math and computer science has elements that are multidisciplinary, cross-disciplinary, and interdisciplinary. One aspect of math and computer science that is super multidisciplinary is AI. AI isn’t just computer science; it involves math, statistics, psychology, ethics, and increasingly law and philosophy. Sometimes those fields just work side by side (multidisciplinary), but sometimes they genuinely have to integrate - especially when talking about fairness, bias, or real-world impact. So in that sense, it isn’t just interdisciplinary by accident; it almost can’t function without it. The problems are too complex, too social, and too embedded in the real world to stay purely technical. AI can also be used to create art, music, poetry, essays, etc.

I see math especially as a heavily cross-disciplinary field. Math can be used to explain so many different fields. There is math in chemistry, music, medicine, etc., and so many fields can be applied to math. Biology becomes computational biology, linguistics becomes NLP, art becomes generative algorithms, and so on. Part of the reason that I chose to study math was the idea that math is everywhere and can be used to explain almost everything. It is a universal language that persists and exists naturally in our universe, and I think that is SO cool.

I see math as a heavily cross-disciplinary field in a different way — less about integration and more about reach. Math can be used to explain so many other fields: there is math in chemistry, music, medicine, biology. Biology becomes computational biology, linguistics becomes NLP, art becomes generative algorithms. Part of the reason I chose to study math was this idea that it is everywhere, a kind of universal language that persists naturally in the structure of our universe. I find that genuinely exciting.

I see interdisciplinary in computer science as the core purpose of the field. We use computers as a method to deepen our understanding of other fields through all technology and the internet. After all, the computers or devices we are all using to engage in this course are made possible by these fields :)
