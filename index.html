01 intro

What does it mean to be on the threshold?

A short note on why this journal exists — and what "liminal" means when applied to a discipline that often resists softness. This is the space between the technical and the lived.

read entry ↓

In our class discussion, someone used the word liminal — the space of thresholds, of being between one thing and another. I couldn't stop thinking about it in the context of computer science.

CS is usually framed as a discipline of precision: correct answers, working code, provable theorems. But so much of what makes it interesting to me lives in the in-between: between a design decision and its human consequences, between a system and the people it shapes, between what we build and why we build it.

The question isn't just "does it compile?" It's "what does it do to people when it runs?"

This journal is my attempt to write from that threshold. The entries here are pieces I wrote over the course of a semester — revised, reflected on, and placed in conversation with each other. They are technical and personal, analytical and uncertain. I hope that feels intentional, not unfinished.

02 piece

The dropdown that decided who you were

On how small design decisions — a form field, a forced binary — can encode assumptions about identity and scale into environments that shape real lives.

read entry ↓

This is the piece I keep coming back to when I think about why this journal exists. It started as a writing assignment about social justice and computer science. It became something more personal. The threshold here is literal: the gap between a design decision made without consideration and the life of a person that decision touches. I wrote this as a letter. I still mean all of it.

To computer scientists:

In computer science, binary is the default.

0s and 1s are the very foundation of every function.

By nature, we are taught to think within rigid boxes with specific notions of truth.

People are made up of many truths.

The body is one kind of truth.

Identity is another.

Most systems are designed under the assumption that those two truths are the same.
But what about when they are not?

The truth of a body I did not choose is kept in databases I have never seen and in code written by people who never considered me. So many of the systems that shape our reality quietly enforce a truth. Right now, many of those systems are built in a way that makes people like me harder to recognize and easier to challenge.

A dropdown menu with two options.

A required field that won't let you proceed.

A scan at the airport going through TSA.

A bathroom.

These are small things. At least they feel small when you build them. Edge cases saved for later.

But systems scale.

And when they do, those small decisions create environments.

In Kansas, a law says that identity must return to the sex assigned at birth. The bathrooms must align with that truth. That someone can take legal action if they feel harmed by your presence alone.

A law like that does not exist in isolation. It relies on systems to enforce it.

In the same way rivers are redirected, contained, and buried beneath cities so that streets can exist where water once flowed freely, identity is redirected, too. Forced into channels that make the system easier to maintain.

But water does not stop existing because you force it down a pipe.

People do not stop existing because a system fails to account for them.

It is easy to think of these problems as strictly social or political issues.
But they are also design problems, which means they have solutions.

That solution, summarized by a single word: consideration.

Design systems with the people who use them in mind.

Design for complexity instead of convenience.

Design for reality instead of assumptions.

Because every dropdown, every policy is making a claim about who is allowed to exist comfortably in the world.

Despite all of this, trans people are still here.

Just as they always have been.

Just as they always will be.

There is something quietly beautiful about that.
To exist without permission and without apology.
To find joy in a world that was not built for you.

It is easy to focus on the things that are hard, defining trans existence with struggle. But what has defined my experiences as a trans person so much more is joy.

It is laughter in spaces that expect silence.

It is a community built where systems have left gaps.

It is choosing yourself, again and again, even when systems refuse to.

Not everyone writes code or passes laws.
But everyone participates in systems, whether they realize it or not.

Anyone can question the defaults.

You can notice who is left out.

And maybe most importantly, you can uplift the people around you.
Make space for everyone to exist loudly.

Because the world changes the same way systems are built, one decision at a time.

03 piece

Reading like a ____________

What happens when you approach a text the way you'd approach a proof or a program? On structure, audience, and the surprising overlap between writing and systems thinking.

read entry ↓

This piece sits at the heart of what this journal is trying to do. Writing about reading — and doing it through the lens of CS and math — helped me see that the two disciplines aren't as separate as they're usually treated. Both are about noticing design, asking why, and learning from what came before you.

Part one

What I liked most about Mike Bunn's How to Read Like a Writer is that it gives a name to something I already do instinctively: considering the choices behind a piece of writing, not just the story or argument itself. Bunn describes reading like a writer as paying attention to how the text was constructed so that you learn how to "build" one for yourself, and that framing really resonated with me. It treats writing less like magic and more like engineering. Something built intentionally, piece by piece, by someone who made a series of decisions.

I feel like this mindset connects strongly to how I already think in math and computer science. In those fields, you're always asking why something was designed a certain way: Why this algorithm? Why this structure? Why this proof strategy? To me, reading like a writer feels similar. It's about noticing the design decisions and thinking through their consequences.

I've definitely read like a writer before, both consciously and unconsciously. It became a bigger habit after AP English, where we spent time analyzing reading as writers — especially in our poetry and personal essays unit. I remember reading Invisible Man with the theme of music in mind, and finding that I got much more out of the book by keeping track of how and why Ralph Ellison used that theme as a tool. We also read personal essays closely in order to find choices we liked and apply those techniques to our college essays. As a result, I often read with the author's intentions in mind — noticing repeated symbols, the pacing of scenes, the way a sentence is shaped.

One idea I've always struggled with is getting caught up in what the author did "on purpose" versus what might have been accidental. But Bunn helped me realize that this doesn't really matter. If something means something to a reader who is reading like a writer, that reader can still learn from it and apply it to their own writing. Even if the author didn't consciously plan it, the effect is still real and still useful as a model.

"When you read like a writer, you are trying to figure out how the text you are reading was constructed so that you learn how to 'build' one for yourself."

To me, this frames reading as part of a long chain of progress: learning from people who came before you, studying their methods, and then adapting or improving on them. You don't start from nothing; you study existing solutions and learn how they work.

Part two — how to read like a computer science and math major

Reading like a computer science and math major means reading with an eye for structure, design choices, and purpose.

First, always look for the design choices. Ask: Why did the author structure this explanation this way? Why introduce this concept before that one? Why use this example instead of another? It's like making sure you define a function before calling it — order matters. Every proof, algorithm, or system is the result of tradeoffs. Writing is no different.

Second, read as if you might have to explain it. When reading something technical — a textbook, paper, or documentation — ask: Where does this get confusing? What assumptions does the author make about the reader? What would I change if I had to teach this?

Third, practice zooming in and zooming out. In CS and math, it's important to remember the bigger picture while also attending to the details.

Zoom in: Look closely at a definition, a paragraph, a line of code, or a single step in a proof. Why is it written this way?
Zoom out: Look at the whole structure. How does this section fit into the chapter? How does this idea support the overall goal?

Fourth, think about context. Who is this written for? What does the author assume you already know? What problem is this trying to solve?

Finally, step away and come back. In math and CS, stepping away from a problem often makes the structure suddenly clearer. The same is true for reading: when you return, you can often see patterns, gaps, and design decisions you missed the first time.

04 piece

Is computer science really about computers?

A look at what CS borrows from other fields — and what those fields reveal about CS. Includes a conversation about AI, authorship, and whether a machine can intend something.

read entry ↓

One of the things I've come to believe over the course of this semester is that computer science is most interesting at its edges — where it bumps up against other disciplines and has to negotiate. This piece started as a discussion post about interdisciplinarity, but it turned into something I keep coming back to: the idea that the problems worth solving are almost always too big for one field.

Part one

After reading sections 1 and 2 of A Time for Interdisciplinarity, I find myself having to agree with the notion that interdisciplinarity — and purposefully teaching it in universities — is critical. The reading argues that interdisciplinary collaboration is a prerequisite for science to contribute to major problems, the so-called "wicked problems," and thus generate real impact from knowledge. I think that all of us here have an advantage in understanding the value of interdisciplinary education, given that we all study at a university that facilitates such learning through combined majors across colleges.

Intentionally learning in an interdisciplinary way teaches us to speak each other's academic languages. Perhaps that feels both obvious and hard. Obvious because collaboration always requires communication, but hard because every discipline has its own assumptions, vocabulary, and standards of proof. Learning from different disciplines shows us how to become learners and synthesizers, not just parrots of theorems and definitions we have memorized. We learn how and why different disciplines have different methods, rather than getting stuck in one way of seeing the world.

Something I found surprising in the reading was the spectrum of terms: multidisciplinarity, interdisciplinarity, transdisciplinarity, cross-disciplinarity, even postdisciplinarity. I didn't realize there were so many distinct modes. I especially liked the idea that interdisciplinarity exists on a continuum — from simple communication between fields all the way to full integration of methods, concepts, and ways of thinking. Distinguishing between them matters because it allows us to be more intentional about how we engage across disciplines and how we communicate when doing so.

Interdisciplinarity isn't just a preference — it's something we need when the problem itself is too big for one field.

Part two

My combined major of math and computer science has elements that are multidisciplinary, cross-disciplinary, and interdisciplinary — often all at once.

AI is the clearest example. It isn't just computer science; it involves math, statistics, psychology, ethics, and increasingly law and philosophy. Sometimes those fields just work side by side, but sometimes they genuinely have to integrate — especially when talking about fairness, bias, or real-world impact. The problems are too complex, too social, and too embedded in the real world to stay purely technical. AI can also be used to create art, music, poetry, essays — which raises its own questions about intention and authorship that I find genuinely hard to resolve.

I see math as a heavily cross-disciplinary field in a different way — less about integration and more about reach. Math can be used to explain so many other fields: there is math in chemistry, music, medicine, biology. Biology becomes computational biology, linguistics becomes NLP, art becomes generative algorithms. Part of the reason I chose to study math was this idea that it is everywhere, a kind of universal language that persists naturally in the structure of our universe. I find that genuinely exciting.

And computer science itself — I think of its interdisciplinarity as its core purpose. We use computers as a method to deepen our understanding of other fields, to connect ideas across distances, to make new kinds of collaboration possible. After all, the devices we're all using to read this are made possible by the intersection of many fields working together.
